Introduction

This report analyzes two critical global development indicators for the year 2010:

  1. GDP per capita - Economic prosperity measure
  2. Maternal mortality ratio - Health outcome indicator (deaths per 100,000 live births)

By examining these indicators at a single point in time, we can understand the global distribution of development and explore potential relationships between economic prosperity and health outcomes.

Data Loading and Preparation

The GDP dataset contains 195 countries with data spanning 301 years, while the maternal mortality dataset covers 189 countries.

Interpretation of the Relationship

The scatterplot reveals a strong negative correlation between GDP per capita and maternal mortality rates in 2010:

  • Correlation coefficient (r) = -0.658: This indicates a strong negative relationship
  • Statistical significance: The p-value < 0.001 confirms this relationship is not due to chance
  • Direction: As GDP per capita increases, maternal mortality rates decrease
  • Strength: The correlation explains approximately 43.3% of the variance in maternal mortality rates

Key Insights:

  1. Economic Development and Health: Higher economic prosperity is strongly associated with better maternal health outcomes
  2. Global Health Disparities: The relationship highlights the significant health inequalities between developed and developing nations
  3. Development Priorities: Economic growth appears to be a crucial factor in improving maternal health, though not the only one
  4. Policy Implications: Development strategies that focus on economic growth may have important positive spillover effects on maternal health outcomes

Limitations:

  • Correlation vs. Causation: This analysis shows association, not causation
  • Other Factors: Maternal mortality is influenced by many factors beyond GDP (healthcare systems, education, cultural factors, etc.)
  • Data Quality: Some countries may have incomplete or inaccurate reporting
  • Logarithmic Transformation: The log scales help visualize the relationship but may mask some nuances in the data

This analysis demonstrates that economic development and maternal health are closely intertwined, suggesting that comprehensive development strategies should consider both economic and health dimensions together.
